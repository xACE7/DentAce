Tooth preparation, as defined by G.V. Black, is divided into two main stages containing a total of nine steps.

Initial Tooth Preparation Stage:

  1. Outline form & initial depth: Defining the external boundaries of the preparation.
  2. Primary Resistance form: Shaping to prevent fracture of the tooth or restoration.
  3. Primary Retention form: Shaping to prevent dislodgement of the restoration.
  4. Convenience form: Providing adequate access and visibility.

Final Tooth Preparation Stage:

  1. Removal of remaining infected dentin: Excavating any soft, carious tissue.
  2. Pulp protection: Applying liners or bases if needed.
  3. Secondary resistance & retention forms: Adding features like grooves or slots for extra support.
  4. Finishing external walls: Smoothing and refining the preparation margins.
  5. Final procedures: Cleaning, inspecting, and sealing the preparation.

The outline form is the first step and involves placing the preparation margins in their final planned positions. This is done while preparing the tooth to an initial, uniform depth. The fundamental principles are:

  • Remove all weakened or unsupported enamel.
  • Include all carious lesions within the outline.
  • Place margins in positions that allow for good finishing of the restoration.

Key features to consider include:

  • Preserving Cuspal Strength: Avoid extending the preparation up the major cuspal inclines.
  • Preserving Marginal Ridge Strength: Keep the preparation edge away from the marginal ridge to prevent fracture.
  • Minimizing Buccolingual Width: A narrow preparation is stronger.
  • Using Enameloplasty: Reshaping shallow, non-carious fissures instead of cutting them away.
  • Connecting Close Lesions: If two defects are less than 0.5 mm apart, they should be joined into one preparation.
  • Restricting Depth: The initial pulpal depth for pit and fissure caries is typically extended just 0.2 mm into dentin.

Primary resistance form refers to the shape and placement of the preparation walls that best enable both the tooth and the restoration to withstand masticatory forces without fracturing.

The key features for achieving good resistance form are:

  • Relatively flat floors: A flat pulpal floor, perpendicular to the long axis of the tooth, helps distribute forces and prevents a "rocking" motion.
  • Box shape: This provides stability and bulk for the restorative material.
  • Inclusion of weakened tooth structure: Any compromised cusps or areas should be included in the preparation and potentially covered by the restoration (cusp capping).
  • Preservation of cusps and marginal ridges: This is a core principle from the outline form that also contributes to resistance.
  • Rounded internal line angles: Sharp angles create stress concentration points. Rounding them reduces the risk of fracture.
  • Adequate thickness of restorative material: The preparation must be deep enough to allow for a sufficient bulk of material. For amalgam, this is a minimum of 1.5-2 mm.
