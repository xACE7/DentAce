Composite materials are highly versatile and have a wide range of applications in modern dentistry. Their indications include:

  • Class I, II, III, IV, and V restorations: Composites can be used for restorations in both anterior and posterior teeth.
  • Foundations or Core Buildups: They are used to build up tooth structure before placing a crown.

  • Esthetic Enhancement: Procedures like creating full veneers or closing diastemas (gaps between teeth).
  • Periodontal Splinting: Used to stabilize mobile teeth by bonding them together.
  • Non-carious Lesions: For restoring areas of tooth wear not caused by decay.
  • Indirect Restorations: Fabricating composite inlays and onlays outside the mouth for later cementation.

While composites are versatile, there are situations where they are not the ideal choice. The primary contraindications relate to three main factors:

  1. Inability to Isolate: Successful bonding requires a clean, dry environment, completely isolated from oral fluids like saliva. If the operating site cannot be properly isolated (ideally with a rubber dam), composite should not be used. Contamination will lead to bond failure.
  2. Heavy Occlusion: If all the biting forces are concentrated on the composite material, it may wear down faster than other materials. For patients with heavy occlusion, bruxism (tooth grinding), or restorations that bear the full occlusal load, a material with higher wear resistance like amalgam might be a better choice.
  3. Restorations Extending onto the Root Surface: Achieving a perfect marginal seal can be difficult when a restoration extends onto the root surface. This can lead to gap formation due to polymerization shrinkage. Using a resin-modified glass ionomer (RMGI) liner in a "sandwich technique" can help reduce this issue.

Advantages of Composite RestorationsÙ…Ø²Ø§ÙŠØ§ Ø­Ø´ÙˆØ§Øª Ø§Ù„ÙƒÙˆÙ…Ø¨ÙˆØ²ÙŠØª

Composite restorations offer numerous benefits over traditional materials like amalgam:

  • Esthetics: This is the most significant advantage. Composites are tooth-colored and can be matched to the patient's natural dentition.
  • Conservative Tooth Preparation: Since composites are bonded to the tooth, there is less need to remove healthy tooth structure for mechanical retention. Preparations can be less extensive and do not require a uniform depth.
  • Less Complex Preparation: Cavity designs are simpler compared to those for amalgam.
  • Low Thermal Conductivity: Composites are good insulators and do not transmit temperature changes as readily as metallic restorations, reducing the risk of post-operative sensitivity.

Acid etching is a critical step developed by Buonocore in 1955. It prepares the tooth surface for micromechanical bonding. The most common agent used is 37% phosphoric acid, available as a gel or liquid.

Effect on Enamel: Etching selectively dissolves the enamel structure, creating microscopic pores (microporosities). This dramatically increases the surface area and surface energy, removes the smear layer, and allows low-viscosity resin to flow in and form resin tags for retention.

Effect on Dentin: Etching removes the smear layer and the hydroxyapatite mineral component, exposing a network of collagen fibrils within the dentin matrix and opening the dentinal tubules.

Etching Techniques:

  • Total-Etch: Applying acid to both enamel and dentin simultaneously for about 15 seconds.
  • Selective-Etch: Etching the enamel first (for 30 seconds) and then applying the acid to the dentin for the final 15 seconds. This provides a stronger enamel bond while protecting the dentin from over-etching.

After etching, the surface is rinsed thoroughly (15 seconds) and gently dried. A properly etched enamel surface will have a characteristic "frosty white" appearance. It is crucial not to over-dry the dentin, as this can cause the collagen network to collapse, preventing proper adhesive penetration.

Adhesive Application:

After etching and drying, a low-viscosity adhesive (bonding agent) is applied, typically in two coats with a microbrush. It flows into the enamel pores and dentinal tubules. On dentin, the adhesive penetrates the exposed collagen network to form a hybrid layer, which is an interlocking zone of resin and collagen that is crucial for dentin bonding. After application, the adhesive is gently air-thinned to evaporate the solvent and then light-cured for about 20 seconds.

Composite Placement (Incremental Layering):

To counteract the effects of polymerization shrinkage, composite is placed in small increments, layer by layer. Each increment should be a maximum of 1-2 mm thick. Each layer is light-cured (e.g., for 20 seconds) before the next is added. This technique reduces overall shrinkage stress and ensures complete curing of the material.

Common layering techniques include:

  • Horizontal Layering: Placing flat layers one on top of another. Can increase shrinkage stress.
  • Oblique Layering: Placing wedge-shaped increments against the cavity walls. This is preferred as it reduces polymerization shrinkage stress.
  • Successive Cusp Buildup: Rebuilding each cusp individually, which helps recreate natural anatomy and reduces finishing time.

Finishing and polishing are the final, crucial steps to ensure the restoration's longevity, esthetics, and health. These procedures can be initiated immediately after the final layer of composite is light-cured.

The goals of finishing and polishing are to:

  • Achieve correct anatomical contours and occlusion.
  • Remove any excess material and smooth the margins.
  • Create a highly smooth, lustrous surface that resists staining and plaque accumulation.

Finishing: This involves gross contouring to establish the final shape. It is typically done with multi-fluted carbide burs or fine-grit diamond burs. Rotary instruments should always be used with water spray and light pressure to avoid overheating the composite and tooth.

Polishing: After finishing, a series of polishing instruments are used to create a high gloss. This is often done with flexible discs, cups, and points that have progressively finer abrasive particles. Systems with multiple grits (coarse to fine) are used sequentially to achieve a mirror-like finish. Microfilled composites are known to achieve the highest level of polish.
