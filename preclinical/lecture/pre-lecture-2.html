Removing caries and preparing teeth are fundamental to restorative dentistry. Initially done with hand instruments, the process was revolutionized by rotary-powered equipment. Hand instruments are primarily made from two materials:

  • Carbon Steel: Harder than stainless steel, providing a keener edge, but it corrodes (rusts) if not protected.
  • Stainless Steel: An alloy that resists corrosion and stays bright, but its cutting edge dulls more quickly during use.

According to Marzouk's classification, hand instruments are categorized into four main groups:

  1. Diagnostic Instruments: Used for examination (e.g., mirrors, probes).
  2. Cutting Instruments: Used for removing tooth structure (e.g., excavators, chisels).
  3. Restoring Instruments: Used for placing restorative materials (e.g., amalgam carriers, condensers).
  4. Finishing and Polishing Instruments: Used to smooth and polish restorations (e.g., burnishers, rubber cups).

Parts of a Hand InstrumentØ£Ø¬Ø²Ø§Ø¡ Ø§Ù„Ø£Ø¯Ø§Ø© Ø§Ù„ÙŠØ¯ÙˆÙŠØ©

A typical hand instrument consists of three main parts:

  • Handle (Shaft): The part grasped by the operator. It is often serrated or textured for a better grip.
  • Shank: Connects the handle to the working end (blade or nib). The shank's design determines the accessibility of the instrument. It can have various angles:
    • Straight: No angles.
    • Mono-angled: One angle.
    • Bin-angled: Two angles.
    • Triple-angled: Three angles.
  • Blade or Nib: The working end of the instrument.
    • For cutting instruments, it is called the Blade, which terminates in a cutting edge.
    • For non-cutting instruments (like condensers), it is called the Nib.

Contra-angling refers to designing the shank with multiple angles (bin- or triple-angled) to bring the working edge closer to the long axis of the handle. This provides better balance and control, preventing the instrument from rotating during use.

Proper grasp and rest techniques are essential for instrument control, stability, and preventing injury.

  • Modified Pen Grasp: The most common grasp. The instrument is held by the pads of the thumb, index, and middle fingers. The middle finger provides pressure and control. A finger rest is crucial for steadying the hand, preventing instrument slippage, and allowing for precise, controlled movements.
