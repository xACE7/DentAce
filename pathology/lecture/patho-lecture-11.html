Osteomyelitis is defined as an inflammation of the bone and bone marrow. This condition is almost always a secondary result of an infection.

It can be classified based on its duration and cause:

  • Acute Osteomyelitis: A sudden onset of infection.
  • Subacute Osteomyelitis: A less severe, more gradual form.
  • Chronic Osteomyelitis: A long-standing infection, which can be further divided into:
    • Non-specific: Caused by common pyogenic bacteria.
    • Specific: Caused by a specific organism, such as Tuberculosis.

Acute pyogenic osteomyelitis is a pus-forming inflammation of the bone and marrow. It is caused by pyogenic (pus-forming) bacteria.

The most common causative organisms depend on the clinical setting:

  • Haemophilus influenzae and Group B streptococci: Common causative agents in neonates.
  • Salmonella: Strongly associated with patients who have sickle cell anemia.
  • Mixed bacteria: Often found in cases resulting from post-traumatic injuries.

Bacteria can reach the bone in several ways:

  • Hematogenous spread: Bacteria travel through the bloodstream from another site of infection.
  • Direct extension: Infection spreads from adjacent soft tissue, such as a skin infection.
  • Direct implantation: Bacteria are introduced directly into the bone, for example, through a compound fracture.

The infection often localizes in the metaphysis of long bones in children due to its high blood supply and unique vascular loop structure. In infants (under 2 years), trans-epiphyseal vessels allow bacteria to reach the epiphysis, making it more dangerous. In older children and adults, the epiphyseal plate is closed, confining the infection to the metaphysis.

The process evolves over time:

  • First 48 hours: Acute inflammation occurs.
  • After 48 hours: An abscess forms, compressing blood vessels.
  • After 1st week: Cytokines and growth factors are released, leading to ischemic necrosis (bone death) and new bone formation. This results in:
    • Sequestrum: The piece of dead bone.
    • Involucrum: The new bone that forms around the sequestrum.

The progression of acute osteomyelitis leads to several distinct pathological features, typically seen in the metaphysis of long bones or the vertebrae.

  • Suppuration & Abscess Formation: The inflammatory response leads to the formation of pus (suppuration), which collects into an abscess. This subperiosteal abscess can lift the periosteum off the bone, cutting off its blood supply.
  • Sequestrum: This is a piece of dead bone resulting from ischemic necrosis caused by the compromised blood supply.
  • Involucrum: This is a shell of new, reactive bone that the periosteum forms around the sequestrum in an attempt to wall off the infection.
  • Cloaca (Draining Sinus): The involucrum may have openings called cloacae, which allow pus to drain from the infected bone to the outside, forming a draining sinus tract through the skin.
