Introduction to NecrosisÙ…Ù‚Ø¯Ù…Ø© Ø¹Ù† Ø§Ù„Ù†Ø®Ø±

Necrosis is defined as the local death of a large group of cells within a living body. It is a major consequence of severe and progressive irreversible cell injury.

The process of necrosis typically involves several steps:

  • Cell Swelling: The cell and its organelles begin to swell.
  • Membrane Rupture: The cell membrane breaks down, causing cellular contents to leak out.
  • Inflammation: The leakage of contents induces an inflammatory response in the surrounding tissue.
  • Enzymatic Digestion: The dead cells are digested by enzymes.

Microscopic Changes:

  • Cytoplasm: Becomes more eosinophilic (stains red) and the cell membrane appears indistinct.
  • Nucleus: Undergoes a sequence of changes:
    1. Pyknosis: The nucleus shrinks and chromatin becomes dense.
    2. Karyorrhexis: The nucleus fragments into multiple small pieces.
    3. Karyolysis: The nucleus completely dissolves.

1. Coagulative Necrosis:

  • This is the most common type of necrosis.
  • Cause: It results from a sudden cut-off of blood supply, known as ischemia.
  • Site: Can occur in all solid organs except the brain.
  • Gross Appearance: The affected area appears firm and opaque white.

  • Characteristic: Involves the digestion of dead cells, transforming the tissue into a liquid, viscous mass.
  • Cause & Site:
    • Common in the Central Nervous System (brain and spinal cord) following ischemic injury.
    • Also occurs in bacterial or fungal infections, leading to the formation of an abscess (pus).
  • Gross Appearance: The area appears as a creamy yellow liquid, commonly known as pus.

3. Caseous Necrosis:

  • The term "caseous" means "cheese-like".
  • Cause: It is characteristic of tuberculosis (TB) infections.
  • Gross Appearance: The necrotic tissue is friable and white, resembling cottage cheese.
  • Microscopic Appearance: There is a complete loss of tissue architecture and cellular details. The tissue appears as a pink, structureless, homogenous mass.

4. Fat Necrosis:

  • This refers to a localized area of fat destruction.
  • Traumatic Fat Necrosis: Caused by trauma to adipose tissue, such as in the breast, leading to the rupture of fat cells.
  • Enzymatic Fat Necrosis: Occurs in acute pancreatitis when released pancreatic enzymes liquefy fat cells in the peritoneal cavity.

5. Fibrinoid Necrosis:

  • Site: Typically seen in the walls of blood vessels (vasculitis).
  • Cause: Often associated with autoimmune diseases.
  • Microscopic Appearance: Characterized by the deposition of a pink, fibrin-like material in the artery walls.

Introduction to ApoptosisÙ…Ù‚Ø¯Ù…Ø© Ø¹Ù† Ø§Ù„Ù…ÙˆØª Ø§Ù„Ù…Ø¨Ø±Ù…Ø¬ Ù„Ù„Ø®Ù„ÙŠØ©

Apoptosis is a form of programmed cell death designed to eliminate unwanted or damaged cells. Unlike necrosis, it typically affects single cells or small groups of cells and does not trigger an inflammatory response.

The process of apoptosis is highly organized and involves the following morphological steps:

  • Cell Shrinkage: The cell becomes smaller and more compact.
  • Nuclear Fragmentation: The nucleus breaks down into fragments.
  • Formation of Apoptotic Bodies: The cell breaks up into small, membrane-bound fragments called apoptotic bodies. These bodies contain portions of the cytoplasm and nucleus.
  • Phagocytosis: These apoptotic bodies are quickly engulfed and cleared by phagocytes, such as macrophages, preventing the leakage of cellular contents.

Apoptosis can be triggered by both normal physiological processes and pathological conditions.

A. Physiological Causes:

  • Embryogenesis: Shaping organs and removing structures like the webbing between fingers.
  • Removing Old Cells: For example, neutrophils in an acute inflammatory response die via apoptosis after their job is done.
  • Eliminating Harmful Cells: Removing self-reactive lymphocytes to prevent autoimmune diseases.

B. Pathological Causes:

  • DNA Damage: Caused by radiation or cytotoxic anticancer drugs, which triggers apoptosis if the damage is beyond repair.
  • Abnormal Cells: Eliminating cells infected by viruses (like HIV) or transplanted cells that are rejected.

Molecular Mechanism:

Apoptosis is controlled by two main pathways:

  1. Intrinsic (Mitochondrial) Pathway: This is the major pathway. Lack of survival signals or DNA damage leads to the activation of pro-apoptotic proteins (Bax, Bak), which punch holes in the mitochondrial membrane. This allows cytochrome c to leak out, activating enzymes called caspases that execute cell death. In healthy cells, anti-apoptotic proteins like Bcl-2 prevent this leakage.
  2. Extrinsic (Death Receptor) Pathway: Activated by external signals binding to "death receptors" on the cell surface.

  • Irreversible cell death occurs in two main forms: necrosis and apoptosis.
  • Necrosis is a "messy" pathological process involving cell swelling, membrane rupture, and inflammation. It has several distinct types:
    • Coagulative: Most common, due to ischemia (e.g., heart attack).
    • Liquefactive: In the brain or in abscesses.

  • Gangrene is necrosis complicated by bacterial putrefaction, leading to a foul smell and black discoloration. Key types include Dry (arterial occlusion) and Moist (arterial/venous occlusion), with Moist Gangrene being a medical emergency.
