Pathologic calcification, also known as Heterotopic Calcification, is the process where calcium salts are deposited in tissues other than bone or enamel. This is an abnormal condition.

Morphological Features:

  • Macroscopic (Gross) appearance: It looks like fine, white, gritty deposits.
  • Microscopic appearance: Under a microscope, it appears as basophilic (blue-colored) granular deposits.

There are two main types of pathologic calcification:

  1. Dystrophic calcification: Occurs in dead or damaged tissue.
  2. Metastatic calcification: Occurs in living, healthy tissue.

Dystrophic CalcificationØ§Ù„ØªÙƒÙ„Ø³ Ø§Ù„Ø­Ø«Ø±ÙŠ

Dystrophic calcification is the deposition of calcium salts specifically in dead or degenerated (injured) tissues. A key feature is that it occurs even when serum calcium levels are normal and there are no issues with calcium metabolism.

Common Conditions Leading to Dystrophic Calcification:

  • Atherosclerosis: Calcification is almost always present in the atheromas of advanced atherosclerosis in large arteries.
  • Damaged Heart Valves: It commonly develops in aging or damaged heart valves.
  • Necrosis: It can be seen in any area of necrosis. For example, a tuberculous lymph node can become as hard as stone due to calcification.

Morphology:

  • Grossly: Calcium salts appear as fine, white granules or clumps that feel gritty.
  • Microscopically: Using an H&E stain, the salts have a basophilic, amorphous, granular appearance, sometimes clumped together.

  • Gastric mucosa
  • Kidneys
  • Lungs

The main driver is a derangement in calcium metabolism, leading to high levels of calcium in the blood that then deposits in otherwise healthy tissues.

Intracellular accumulations occur when metabolic problems in cells lead to the abnormal buildup of various substances inside the cytoplasm or nucleus. These accumulations can be harmless, but sometimes they are severely toxic.

Mechanisms of Accumulation:

  • Overproduction: The substance is produced faster than it can be removed.
  • Inadequate Metabolism: The cell cannot break down the substance properly.
  • Poor Removal: A lack of enzyme action prevents the substance from being removed.

Fatty Change (Steatosis) is a specific type of accumulation involving an abnormal buildup of triglycerides (fat) within parenchymal cells. It is most often seen in the liver, but can also occur in the heart, skeletal muscle, and kidneys.

Causes of Fatty Liver:

  • Excess Fat: Obesity, Diabetes mellitus.
  • Liver Cell Damage: Alcohol, toxins, steroids, starvation, protein malnutrition, and chronic illness can impair the liver's ability to metabolize fat.

Morphology of Fatty Liver:

  • Grossly: The liver is enlarged, yellow, and feels greasy.
  • Microscopically: Lipid vacuoles appear in the cytoplasm of hepatocytes, pushing the nucleus to the periphery and giving the cell an empty look.
