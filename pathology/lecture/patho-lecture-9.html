Healing is the body's response to injury, aiming to restore normal structure and function. It involves two main processes: Regeneration and Repair by Scar Formation.

  • Regeneration: The ideal outcome where damaged tissue is replaced by specialized (parenchymal) cells of the same type. It results in complete restoration and occurs after small injuries in tissues with proliferating cells.
  • Repair by Scar Formation: Occurs when damaged tissue is replaced by connective tissue (fibrosis), resulting in a scar. This happens after large injuries or in tissues where cells cannot proliferate.

Cell Proliferative Capacity:

  • Labile cells: Continuously proliferate throughout life (e.g., skin, GI tract mucosa).
  • Stable cells: Have a low level of replication but can proliferate in response to injury (e.g., liver, kidney).
  • Permanent cells: Cannot proliferate after birth (e.g., nerves, cardiac muscle).

Tissue regeneration is the process of replacing damaged components to return the tissue to a normal state. It involves four key mechanisms:

  • Cell Migration: Epithelial and inflammatory cells move into the injured area.

    • Basement membrane: Primarily composed of Collagen Type IV.

Wound healing is a dynamic process that follows a predictable timeline through four overlapping phases:

  1. Hemostasis (Immediate): Platelets aggregate at the injury site to form a fibrin clot. This stops the bleeding and provides a temporary matrix for cell migration.
  2. Inflammatory Phase (Day 1-4): Neutrophils arrive first to clear debris and bacteria. They are followed by macrophages, which continue debridement and release crucial cytokines and growth factors to orchestrate the next phase.
  3. Proliferative Phase (Day 4-21): This phase is characterized by the formation of granulation tissue. Key events include angiogenesis (new blood vessel formation), collagen deposition by fibroblasts, and epithelialization (regrowth of the epidermis).
  4. Maturation / Remodeling (Day 21 - 2 years): The collagen is reorganized and cross-linked, and the wound contracts. The scar tissue strengthens, eventually reaching about 80% of the original tissue's tensile strength.

Healing by primary intention (or primary union) occurs in small, clean, uninfected wounds where the edges are brought together, such as a surgical incision.

Characteristics:

  • Wound Type: Clean, small wound.
  • Edges: Opposed (close together).
  • Gap: Minimal or no gap.
  • Damage: Little tissue damage.
  • Process: The healing process is rapid, with minimal granulation tissue and scarring.
    1. A small clot forms.
    2. Inflammation is minimal.
    3. Epithelial cells quickly grow across the small gap.
    4. A small amount of granulation tissue forms and matures.
    5. The scar eventually fades (scar off / resolution).
  • Outcome: Complete return to normal structure and function with a fine-line scar.

Healing by secondary intention occurs in large, contaminated, or gaping wounds where the edges are not opposed.

Characteristics:

  • Wound Type: Large, often contaminated wound.
  • Edges: Separated.
  • Gap: A wide gap that must be filled.
  • Damage: More extensive tissue damage.
  • Process: The healing process is slower and more complex.
    • A large clot forms, and inflammation is more intense.
    • A large amount of granulation tissue is needed to fill the defect.
    • Epithelial migration is slow and often incomplete.
    • Wound contraction occurs, where specialized cells called myofibroblasts pull the edges of the wound together, reducing its size.
  • Outcome: A permanent, often large, and contracted scar.
