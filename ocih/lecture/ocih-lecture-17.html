This process kicks off during the advanced bell stage of tooth development, but only after the initial formation of dentin, a process called dentinogenesis. Amelogenesis is a complex process divided into two primary stages:

1. Secretion Stage (Matrix Deposition): In this first phase, ameloblasts secrete enamel matrix proteins, which form an organic scaffold. This initial matrix is only partially mineralized (about 30%).

The life of an ameloblast is a dynamic journey. Based on its function, this journey is divided into five distinct stages:

  • 1. Presecretory Stage: The cell prepares for its job. This includes the Morphogenetic Phase (shaping the crown) and the Differentiation Phase (becoming a specialized ameloblast).
  • 2. Secretory (Formative) Stage: The cell actively secretes the enamel matrix.
  • 3. Transitional Stage: A brief phase where the cell prepares to switch from secretion to maturation.
  • 4. Maturation Stage: The cell's primary role is to mineralize the enamel.
  • 5. Protective Stage: After enamel formation is complete, the cell becomes part of a protective layer over the crown until eruption.

Amelogenesis Imperfecta (AI) is not a single disease but a group of inherited genetic conditions that cause defects in enamel formation. These defects can affect the structure, clinical appearance, and composition of enamel in all teeth, in both primary and permanent dentitions.

Amelogenesis is the formation of enamel by ameloblasts, a process that occurs after dentinogenesis begins.

  • It involves a **Secretory Stage** where a protein matrix is laid down, featuring the development of **Tomes' process** to form enamel rods.
  • This is followed by a long **Maturation Stage**, where proteins are removed and minerals are added, hardening the enamel to 96% mineral content. This stage is characterized by ameloblasts **modulating** between ruffled-ended (mineral pumping) and smooth-ended (waste removal) forms.
  • The life cycle of the ameloblast concludes with a **Protective Stage**, forming the **Reduced Enamel Epithelium (REE)** that covers the tooth until eruption.
  • Disruptions to this process can lead to genetic disorders like **Amelogenesis Imperfecta**, affecting enamel quality and quantity.
