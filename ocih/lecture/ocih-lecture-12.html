The Temporomandibular Joint (TMJ) is one of the most complex joints in the body. It's where the mandible (lower jaw) articulates with the cranium, specifically the temporal bone.

This joint is unique because it combines two types of movements:

  • Hinging movement: This is a simple open-and-close motion, like a door hinge. This makes it a Ginglymoid joint.
  • Gliding movement: This allows the jaw to move forward, backward, and side-to-side. This makes it an Arthrodial joint.

Because it performs both actions, the TMJ is technically classified as a Ginglymoarthrodial joint. It is also a synovial joint, meaning it contains a fluid-filled cavity that lubricates movement.

The TMJ's complex movements are made possible by its unique anatomy. A key component is the articular disc (or meniscus), a piece of dense fibrous tissue located between the head of the mandible and the temporal bone.

This disc divides the joint into two separate synovial compartments:

  • Upper Compartment (Meniscotemporal): Located between the upper surface of the disc and the temporal bone. The primary movement here is translation (gliding).
  • Lower Compartment (Meniscomandibular): Located between the disc and the head of the mandible. The primary movement here is rotation (hinging).

This two-compartment system allows the TMJ to perform both rotation and gliding simultaneously, enabling complex functions like chewing.

Opening the mouth is not a single motion but a combination of two distinct movements that occur in sequence:

1. Rotation (Hinging): During the initial phase of opening the mouth (the first 20-25 mm), the head of the mandible simply rotates on the undersurface of the articular disc. This is a pure hinge-like movement and occurs primarily in the lower compartment of the TMJ.

2. Translation (Gliding): To open the mouth wider, the rotation is followed by a gliding movement. The articular disc and the head of the mandible slide forward and downward together, along the articular eminence of the temporal bone. This translation occurs primarily in the upper compartment.

Closing the mouth involves reversing these movements: first translation backward, then rotation to bring the teeth together.
