Salivary glands are exocrine glands that produce saliva and release it into the oral cavity through ducts. They are classified into two main types: major and minor glands.

  • Major Salivary Glands: These are three pairs of large, encapsulated glands that produce about 90% of saliva. They are the Parotid, Submandibular, and Sublingual glands.
  • Minor Salivary Glands: These are hundreds of small, unencapsulated glands scattered throughout the oral mucosa (lips, cheeks, palate, tongue). They produce the remaining 10% of saliva and provide continuous lubrication.

The primary functions of saliva include maintaining oral moisture, initiating digestion, and protecting the oral cavity.

The Parotid Gland is the largest of the major salivary glands. It is shaped like an inverted pyramid and has several key anatomical borders and surfaces.

Location & Borders:

  • Superiorly (Above): It is situated just below the external auditory meatus and the zygomatic arch.
  • Inferiorly (Below): It extends below the angle of the mandible.
  • Anteriorly (In front): It overlaps the posterior part of the masseter muscle.
  • Posteriorly (Behind): It overlaps the anterior part of the sternocleidomastoid muscle.

Surfaces:

  • It has three surfaces: superficial (lateral), anteromedial, and posteromedial.
  • It has two main borders: anterior and posterior.

The parotid gland is surrounded by important structures and is enclosed by two capsules.

Surface Relations:

  • Lateral (Superficial) Surface: This surface is related to the skin, superficial fascia, and superficial parotid lymph nodes.
  • Anteromedial Surface: This surface is related to the mandible, masseter muscle, and medial pterygoid muscle.
  • Posteromedial Surface: This surface is related to the mastoid and styloid processes and their attached muscles. The facial nerve pierces this surface to enter the gland. It is also related to the carotid sheath and its contents (Internal Carotid Artery, Internal Jugular Vein, and lower 4 cranial nerves).

Capsules:

  • Inner (True) Capsule: A thin layer of connective tissue that is part of the gland itself.
  • Outer (False) Capsule: A tough, fibrous layer derived from the investing layer of the deep cervical fascia. This capsule is very dense and inelastic.

Parotid (Stensen's) Duct:

The duct emerges from the anterior border of the gland, travels forward across the masseter muscle, and then turns medially. It pierces four structures to enter the mouth:

  1. Buccal pad of fat
  2. Buccopharyngeal fascia
  3. Buccinator muscle
  4. Mucous membrane of the cheek

It opens into the vestibule of the mouth on a small elevation called the parotid papilla, located opposite the upper second molar tooth.

Nerve Supply:

  • Parasympathetic (Secretory): Fibers originate from the glossopharyngeal nerve (CN IX), travel via the tympanic plexus, lesser petrosal nerve, synapse in the otic ganglion, and finally reach the gland via the auriculotemporal nerve to stimulate saliva production.
  • Sympathetic (Vasomotor): Fibers come from the plexus around the external carotid artery, regulating blood flow.
  • Sensory: The auriculotemporal nerve provides sensation to the gland itself, while the great auricular nerve provides sensation to the capsule.

The Submandibular Gland is located in the submandibular triangle. It is J-shaped and consists of two parts that wrap around the posterior border of the mylohyoid muscle.

  • Superficial Part: The larger part, located superficial to the mylohyoid muscle.
    • Laterally: Related to the submandibular fossa of the mandible.
    • Medially: Related to the mylohyoid muscle.
    • Superficially: Covered by skin, fascia, platysma, and submandibular lymph nodes.
  • Deep Part: The smaller part, located deep to the mylohyoid muscle.
    • Laterally: Mylohyoid muscle.
    • Medially: Hyoglossus muscle.
    • Superiorly: Lingual nerve and submandibular ganglion.
    • Inferiorly: Hypoglossal nerve.

Like the parotid, it has a true fibrous capsule and a false capsule from the deep cervical fascia.

Submandibular (Wharton's) Duct:

This long duct emerges from the anterior end of the deep part of the gland. It runs forward between the mylohyoid and hyoglossus muscles, and then between the sublingual gland and genioglossus muscle.

A key relationship is that the lingual nerve hooks around the duct, starting lateral, crossing inferiorly, and ending medial to it. The duct opens into the floor of the mouth on the sublingual papilla, at the side of the frenulum of the tongue.

Nerve Supply:

  • Parasympathetic (Secretory): Fibers originate from the facial nerve (CN VII) via its chorda tympani branch. These fibers join the lingual nerve and synapse in the submandibular ganglion before supplying the gland.
  • Sympathetic (Vasomotor): Fibers come from the plexus around the facial artery.
  • Sensory: Provided by the lingual nerve.

Sublingual Gland:

This is the smallest of the three major salivary glands. It is almond-shaped and lies in the floor of the mouth, beneath the mucous membrane, within the sublingual fold.

  • Ducts: Unlike the other major glands, it does not have a single large duct. Instead, it has 8 to 20 small ducts that open directly into the floor of the mouth on the sublingual fold.
  • Nerve Supply: It shares the same parasympathetic, sympathetic, and sensory nerve supply as the submandibular gland (from the chorda tympani, facial artery plexus, and lingual nerve, respectively).

Minor Salivary Glands:

There are hundreds of these glands distributed throughout the oral cavity. They are named based on their location and have different secretion types:

  • Labial (lips): Mixed (serous and mucous)
  • Buccal (cheeks): Mixed
  • Palatine (palate): Purely mucous
  • Lingual (tongue): Anterior (mixed), Middle (serous), Posterior (mucous)
