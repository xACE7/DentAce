The periodontium provides the essential support needed to keep teeth functional. It is a complex structure made up of four main components that work together to anchor the teeth within the jaw.

  • Gingiva: The soft tissue that covers the alveolar bone and surrounds the neck of the tooth.
  • Periodontal Ligament (PDL): A group of specialized connective tissue fibers that attach the tooth to the alveolar bone.
  • Cementum: A hard, calcified tissue that covers the root of the tooth, providing attachment for the PDL fibers.
  • Alveolar Bone: The bone of the jaw that forms the sockets for the teeth.

The Gingiva: Anatomy and TypesØ§Ù„Ù„Ø«Ø©: Ø§Ù„ØªØ´Ø±ÙŠØ­ ÙˆØ§Ù„Ø£Ù†ÙˆØ§Ø¹

The gingiva is divided into several distinct areas based on its location and function.

  • Marginal Gingiva (Free Gingiva): This is the unattached, terminal edge of the gingiva that surrounds the tooth like a collar. It forms the soft tissue wall of the gingival sulcus.
  • Gingival Sulcus: A shallow, V-shaped space around the tooth, located between the free gingiva and the tooth surface. In a healthy state, its depth is typically 2-3 mm when measured with a periodontal probe.
  • Attached Gingiva: This part is firm, resilient, and tightly bound to the underlying periosteum of the alveolar bone. It is separated from the alveolar mucosa by the mucogingival junction (MGJ).
  • Interdental Gingiva (Papilla): The part of the gingiva that occupies the space between two adjacent teeth. It can be pyramidal in shape or have a "col" form, which is a valley-like depression connecting the facial and lingual papillae.

If a diastema (space between teeth) exists, the interdental papilla is absent, and the gingiva forms a smooth, rounded surface over the bone.

The Alveolar Process and BoneØ§Ù„Ù†Ø§ØªØ¦ Ø§Ù„Ø³Ù†Ø®ÙŠ ÙˆØ§Ù„Ø¹Ø¸Ù…

The alveolar process is the part of the maxilla and mandible that forms and supports the tooth sockets (alveoli). It consists of two main parts.

  1. Alveolar Bone Proper: This is a thin layer of dense bone that lines the tooth socket. It is also known as the cribriform plate because it has many small openings for blood vessels and nerves. Radiographically, it appears as the lamina dura.
  2. Supporting Alveolar Bone: This bone surrounds the alveolar bone proper and provides the main support. It has two components:
    • Cortical Plates: These are compact bone plates that form the outer (facial) and inner (lingual) surfaces of the alveolar process.
    • Spongy Bone (Trabecular Bone): This is the cancellous bone located between the alveolar bone proper and the cortical plates.

The coronal margin of the alveolar process is called the alveolar crest.

Dental radiographs are essential for evaluating the structures of the periodontium that are not visible clinically, especially the bone.

  • Alveolar Crest: In a healthy state, the alveolar crest is located 1.5-2 mm apical to the cementoenamel junction (CEJ) of adjacent teeth. It forms a sharp angle where it meets the lamina dura. A rounded crest can be an early sign of periodontal disease.
  • Lamina Dura: Appears as a dense, radiopaque (white) line surrounding the tooth root. It is continuous with the alveolar crest.
  • Periodontal Ligament (PDL) Space: This appears as a thin, radiolucent (dark) space between the lamina dura and the tooth root. It is typically widest at the crown and apex and narrowest in the middle of the root.

Radiographs also help visualize patterns of bone loss in disease, such as horizontal bone loss (crest height is reduced but parallel to a line between CEJs) and vertical/angular bone loss (bone loss is greater on one side of the tooth, forming a V-shaped defect).

A thorough clinical examination of the gingiva is crucial for assessing periodontal health. The gingiva must be dried before observation to see fine details. The process involves several steps:

  • Visual Examination: Observing the gingiva directly for any changes.
  • Instrumentation: Using tools like a periodontal probe to explore and measure.
  • Palpation: Applying firm but gentle pressure to check the resilience and consistency of the tissue.

Key features to assess during the examination include:

  1. Color: The shade and uniformity of the gingiva.
  2. Size: Whether the gingiva is enlarged or normal.
  3. Contour: The shape of the gingival margins and interdental papillae.
  4. Consistency: The firmness or softness of the tissue.
  5. Surface Texture: The presence or absence of stippling.
  6. Position: The level of the gingival margin in relation to the tooth.
  7. Bleeding on Probing (BOP): Whether the gingiva bleeds when gently probed.
  8. Pain: Any discomfort experienced by the patient during the examination.

Changes in Gingival Disease (Gingivitis)Ø§Ù„ØªØºÙŠØ±Ø§Øª ÙÙŠ Ø£Ù…Ø±Ø§Ø¶ Ø§Ù„Ù„Ø«Ø© (Ø§Ù„ØªÙ‡Ø§Ø¨ Ø§Ù„Ù„Ø«Ø©)

Gingivitis is inflammation of the gingiva, and it causes visible changes that contrast with the features of health. These changes are key diagnostic signs.

  • Color Change: The first sign is often a change from pink to red or bluish-red (cyanotic) due to increased blood flow (hyperemia) and stagnant blood.
  • Size Change: Inflammation causes swelling or edema, leading to gingival enlargement.
  • Contour Change: The fine "knife-edge" margin becomes rounded or rolled. The interdental papillae may become bulbous (swollen and rounded), blunted (flat), or cratered.
  • Consistency Change: The tissue loses its firmness and becomes soft, spongy, and edematous. It pits on pressure and is easily deflected from the tooth. In chronic cases, it can become firm and fibrotic due to collagen production.
  • Texture Change: The stippling is often lost, and the surface becomes smooth and shiny due to the swelling of the connective tissue.
  • Bleeding: The tissue bleeds easily upon gentle probing (BOP) due to ulceration of the sulcular epithelium.

The normal periodontium is a functional unit composed of the gingiva, periodontal ligament, cementum, and alveolar bone. A thorough understanding of its clinical and radiographic features in health is the foundation for diagnosing disease.

  • Gingiva: Coral pink, firm, stippled, with knife-edge margins and pointed papillae. No bleeding on probing.

  • Gingiva: Red/bluish-red, soft/edematous, smooth/shiny, with rolled margins and bulbous or blunted papillae. Bleeding on probing is present.
