The Temporomandibular joint (TMJ) is one of the most complex joints in the body. It's where the mandible (lower jaw) articulates with the cranium, specifically the temporal bone of the skull.

It is classified as a ginglymoarthrodial joint, which means it has two distinct types of movement:

  • Ginglymoid (Hinge) Movement: This is a rotational movement, primarily responsible for the initial opening and closing of the mouth.
  • Arthrodial (Gliding) Movement: This is a translational or sliding movement, where the condyle and disc slide forward and backward. This allows for protrusion and retraction of the jaw.

The TMJ is also a synovial joint, meaning it is enclosed in a fibrous capsule and contains synovial fluid for lubrication.

The development of the TMJ is a phased process that begins early in intrauterine life and continues after birth.

  • 6th-8th Week IUL: The TMJ originates from the first pharyngeal arch, specifically from Meckel's cartilage.
  • 8th-10th Week: Two distinct areas of mesenchymal condensation, the condylar and temporal blastemas, form. The condylar blastema becomes the mandibular condyle, and the temporal blastema ossifies to form part of the temporal bone.
  • 10th-12th Week: The mesenchyme between the two blastemas splits, forming the upper and lower synovial cavities. This process is called cavitation.
  • 12th Week: The remaining mesenchyme condenses to form the articular disc.
  • 12th-20th Week: The joint capsule, synovial membrane, and ligaments develop around the joint.
  • Postnatal Development: The TMJ continues to remodel after birth, influenced by factors like tooth eruption and mastication. It typically achieves its adult form by adolescence, around 16-20 years of age.

The Articular DiscØ§Ù„Ù‚Ø±Øµ Ø§Ù„Ù…ÙØµÙ„ÙŠ

The articular disc (or meniscus) is a biconcave structure made of dense fibrous connective tissue. It is located between the mandibular condyle and the mandibular fossa, dividing the joint into two separate synovial compartments.

  • Structure: In the sagittal view, the disc has three distinct regions: a thick anterior band, a thickest posterior band, and a much thinner intermediate zone. In a healthy joint, the condyle rests on this thin intermediate zone.
  • Composition: The central area of the disc is avascular (no blood vessels) and aneural (no nerves), while the periphery is slightly innervated.
  • Compartments: The disc divides the joint into a superior (upper) compartment and an inferior (lower) compartment. The upper compartment is responsible for translational (gliding) movements, and the lower compartment is for rotational (hinge) movements.
  • Functions: The disc helps to stabilize the joint, reduce mechanical wear, absorb shock, and facilitate lubrication.

The inner surface of the TMJ capsule is lined with a specialized tissue called the synovial membrane. This membrane produces synovial fluid, a clear, viscous fluid that fills the upper and lower joint cavities.

Ligaments are tough, fibrous bands that connect bones and serve to passively limit and restrict extreme joint movements. The TMJ is supported by three functional ligaments and two accessory ligaments.

Functional Ligaments:

  • Collateral (Discal) Ligaments: These attach the medial and lateral edges of the articular disc to the corresponding poles of the condyle. Their function is to restrict the movement of the disc away from the condyle.
  • Capsular Ligament: This is a fibrous membrane that completely encloses the TMJ. Its primary function is to contain the synovial fluid.
  • Temporomandibular Ligament: This is the main suspensory ligament of the joint, located on the lateral side. It limits excessive dropping of the condyle and prevents posterior displacement of the condyle and disc.

Accessory Ligaments:

  • Sphenomandibular Ligament: Runs from the spine of the sphenoid bone to the lingula of the mandible. It does not have a significant limiting effect on normal mandibular movements.
  • Stylomandibular Ligament: Runs from the styloid process to the angle of the mandible. It primarily limits excessive protrusive movements.

Primary Muscles of Mastication:

Different imaging modalities are used to visualize the hard and soft tissue components of the TMJ, each with specific advantages.
