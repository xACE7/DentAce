Diseases affecting the Maxillary Sinus are broadly categorized based on their origin. Understanding this classification is the first step in diagnosis and treatment planning.

Intrinsic Diseases: These originate from within the sinus itself. They primarily involve the tissues lining the sinus.

  • Inflammatory diseases: Includes Mucositis, Sinusitis, Polyps, and Antroliths.
  • Neoplasms (Tumors): Can be benign (like Osteomas) or malignant (like Squamous cell carcinoma).

Extrinsic Diseases: These originate from structures outside the sinus but affect it due to proximity. They often relate to dental issues.

  • Inflammatory diseases: Such as Periostitis.
  • Odontogenic Cysts and Tumors: Cysts and tumors arising from tooth-forming tissues.
  • Traumatic Injuries: Including fractures or displacement of dental structures into the sinus.

MucositisØ§Ù„ØªÙ‡Ø§Ø¨ Ø§Ù„ØºØ´Ø§Ø¡ Ø§Ù„Ù…Ø®Ø§Ø·ÙŠ (Mucositis)

Mucositis is the inflammation and thickening of the mucous membrane lining the paranasal sinuses. This lining is composed of respiratory epithelium.

Normally, the mucous membrane is about 1 mm thick. However, when it becomes inflamed due to an infection or an allergic reaction, its thickness can increase dramatically by 10 to 15 times, reaching over 3 mm.

  • Causes: Allergic conditions, periodontal disease, and periapical disease are common triggers.
  • Clinical Features: It is usually asymptomatic and is often discovered incidentally on a routine radiograph.
  • Radiographic Features: On a periapical radiograph, it appears as a radiopaque (white) band that runs parallel to the bony wall of the sinus.
  • Treatment: The primary treatment is to identify and remove the underlying cause (e.g., treating the periodontal disease).

Sinusitis is a general term for inflammation of the mucosa of any sinus. When it specifically affects the maxillary sinus, it is called Maxillary Sinusitis.

If the inflammation involves all paranasal sinuses simultaneously, the condition is known as Pansinusitis.

Maxillary sinusitis is classified based on its duration:

  • Acute: Lasts up to 4 weeks.
  • Subacute: Lasts from 4 to 12 weeks.
  • Chronic: Lasts for over 12 weeks.

The etiology is varied and can include allergic reactions, viral insults, and bacterial infections. Common bacteria involved are Haemophilus influenzae and Streptococcus pneumoniae. Dental infections, particularly from infected maxillary premolars and first molars, are also a significant cause.

Acute & Subacute SinusitisØ§Ù„ØªÙ‡Ø§Ø¨ Ø§Ù„Ø¬ÙŠÙˆØ¨ Ø§Ù„Ø£Ù†ÙÙŠØ© Ø§Ù„Ø­Ø§Ø¯ ÙˆØªØ­Øª Ø§Ù„Ø­Ø§Ø¯

Acute Sinusitis is characterized by a rapid onset of symptoms. Subacute Sinusitis represents an intermediate stage between acute and chronic, where symptoms persist but may be less severe.

Clinical Features of Acute Sinusitis:

  • A sense of fullness and pain over the cheek, which worsens when bending forward.
  • Pain over the upper jaw, which may be referred to the gums or teeth (maxillary premolars and molars).
  • Nasal discharge and blockage (rhinitis).
  • Tenderness when tapping over the anterior wall of the sinus.
  • Constitutional symptoms like fever, malaise, and body aches.

Diagnosis:

  • Water's (Occipitomental) View Radiograph: This is a key diagnostic tool. It may show an air-fluid level, where fluid appears radiopaque in the lower part of the sinus with a distinct horizontal border.

Chronic Sinusitis is an infection that lasts for months or even years. It often develops when acute sinusitis fails to resolve completely, possibly due to inadequate therapy.

Factors contributing to chronic sinusitis include impaired sinus drainage and persistent mucosal changes. Impaired drainage can be caused by:

  • Nasal Polyps
  • Deviated Nasal Septum (DNS)
  • Adenoids or Tumors
  • Allergies

Clinical Features:

  • Symptoms are often nonspecific and pain is usually absent, unlike in acute sinusitis.
  • A key feature is a foul-smelling purulent discharge.
  • Patients may experience nasal blockage and a change in voice due to loss of resonance.
  • Insomnia can also be a symptom.

An Oroantral Fistula (OAF), also known as an oroantral communication, is an unnatural pathway between the oral cavity and the maxillary sinus. It's a common complication related to the maxillary posterior teeth.

Etiology (Causes):

  • Tooth Extraction: The most common cause, especially the extraction of upper molars whose roots are close to or perforate the sinus floor.
  • Instrumentation: Blind or forceful instrumentation during extraction can push a root tip through the sinus floor.
  • Trauma: Facial trauma or fractures involving the maxilla.
  • Pathology: Other causes include osteomyelitis, malignancy, or deep fungal infections destroying the bone between the mouth and sinus.

Signs and Symptoms:

  • Regurgitation of liquids: Fluids may pass from the mouth into the nose when drinking.
  • Entry of food particles into the antrum (sinus).
  • Unilateral epistaxis: Bleeding from the nose on one side, as blood from the extraction site fills the sinus and drains out the nose.
  • A patient may report air passing from the nose to the mouth when they blow their nose.

Accidents during dental procedures can lead to the displacement of teeth, roots, or dental materials into the maxillary sinus. This is a significant extrinsic cause of sinus pathology.

  • Teeth or Roots: A root tip of a maxillary molar can be accidentally pushed into the sinus during a difficult extraction, especially if excessive force is used. Ectopic teeth can also naturally be present within the sinus.
  • Dental Materials: Overextension of materials like root canal sealers, cements, gutta-percha (GP), or silver cones beyond the tooth's apex can introduce foreign bodies into the sinus.

Consequences:

The presence of these foreign bodies can block sinus drainage and harbor bacteria, leading to odontogenic maxillary sinusitis. If a root remains in the sinus for a long time, it will almost certainly cause chronic sinusitis.

Radiographic Features: The displaced object (e.g., a root tip) will appear as a radiopaque mass, often near the floor of the sinus. Radiographs like occlusal views or a Water's projection are used for localization. A break in the continuity of the sinus floor may also be visible.

Management: Surgical removal of the foreign body is necessary, often via a Caldwell-Luc procedure to gain adequate access to the sinus for retrieval.

The maxillary sinus has a very close anatomical relationship with the orbit, alveolar ridge, and especially the roots of the maxillary posterior teeth. This proximity means that diseases originating in one structure can easily affect the other, leading to confusing and overlapping symptoms.

A thorough understanding of this relationship is essential for any clinician performing dental procedures on maxillary posterior teeth. Preoperative assessment, typically with radiographs, is critical to identify risks such as thin sinus floors or long roots extending into the sinus.

Key takeaways include:

  • Sinus diseases can be intrinsic (from the sinus) or extrinsic (often from teeth).
  • Acute sinusitis presents with pain and pressure, while chronic sinusitis is often painless but has a foul discharge.
  • Dental procedures are a common cause of oroantral fistulas and displacement of foreign bodies into the sinus.
