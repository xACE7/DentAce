Saliva is a watery, slightly viscous fluid that constantly keeps the oral cavity moist. It is secreted by both major and minor salivary glands and forms a protective film that coats the teeth and mucous membranes.

The secretion of saliva is a critical bodily function controlled by a dedicated salivary center in the brain.

Without saliva, a condition known as Xerostomia (dry mouth), several problems arise. These include significant difficulty in speaking, chewing (mastication), and swallowing. The oral environment becomes prone to mucosal infections and a rapid, aggressive form of tooth decay known as rampant caries.

Saliva is produced by three pairs of major salivary glands and numerous minor glands. Each major gland contributes a different amount and type of saliva to the total volume.

  • Submandibular Glands (60%): Located under the floor of the mouth, these are the primary producers of saliva. They secrete a mixed seromucous saliva, which contains both watery (serous) components like enzymes and viscous (mucous) components like mucins.
  • Parotid Glands (30%): The largest salivary glands, located in the cheeks. They produce a purely serous (watery) saliva. This secretion is rich in enzymes like amylase and various proteins.
  • Sublingual Glands (5%): The smallest of the major glands, found under the tongue. They produce a predominantly mucous saliva, which is thick and viscous due to its high mucin content.

Saliva provides continuous protection to the oral cavity through several mechanisms, acting as a physical barrier, lubricant, and chemical buffer.

  1. Mechanical Washing: The constant flow of saliva acts like a natural mouth rinse. It flushes away food debris and non-adherent bacteria, preventing them from accumulating on oral surfaces.
  2. Lubrication: Thanks to its water and mucin content, saliva keeps the oral tissues moist. This lubrication protects against mechanical irritation from food and prevents the tissues from drying out (desiccation).
  3. Pellicle Formation: Special salivary glycoproteins bind to the surface of teeth, forming a thin, protective layer called the salivary pellicle. This membrane acts as a diffusion barrier, protecting the enamel from acids.
  4. Buffering Action: Saliva contains bicarbonates and phosphates, which act as powerful buffers. They neutralize acids produced by plaque bacteria after sugar consumption, thereby preventing the pH from dropping to a level that would dissolve enamel.
