The Incremental Lines of Retzius are brownish bands seen in ground sections of enamel. They illustrate the successive, layered deposition of enamel matrix during crown formation.

In a longitudinal section, they appear as oblique lines running from the dentino-enamel junction (DEJ) towards the tooth surface. In a cross-section, they look like concentric rings, similar to the growth rings of a tree.

A broadening of these lines can indicate a metabolic disturbance that occurred during the time of matrix formation.

Neonatal LineØ§Ù„Ø®Ø· Ø§Ù„ÙˆÙ„Ø§Ø¯ÙŠ

The Neonatal Line is a particularly accentuated Incremental Line of Retzius that marks the boundary between enamel formed before birth (prenatal) and after birth (postnatal). This line is found in all deciduous (baby) teeth and the cusps of the first permanent molars.

It is caused by the abrupt change in environment and nutrition that occurs at birth. Prenatal enamel is typically better developed and more homogenous than postnatal enamel because the fetus develops in a stable, protected environment with a consistent supply of nutrients.

Hunter-Schreger Bands (HSB) are an optical phenomenon seen in longitudinal ground sections of enamel when viewed under oblique reflected light. They appear as alternating light and dark bands.

These bands are not true structures but are caused by the regular, alternating change in direction of adjacent groups of enamel rods. The light bands (parazones) correspond to rods cut longitudinally, while the dark bands (diazones) represent rods cut in cross-section. They originate at the DEJ and extend about two-thirds of the way through the enamel.

Enamel Tufts are hypocalcified, ribbon-like structures that start at the DEJ and extend into the enamel for about one-third of its thickness. They are named for their tufted, branching appearance. Tufts form due to abrupt changes in the direction of enamel rod groups and contain a higher concentration of enamel proteins.

Enamel Lamellae are thin, leaf-like defects that extend from the enamel surface towards or sometimes into the dentin. They are also hypocalcified and can be thought of as cracks filled with organic material. They may form during development due to tension (Type A, B) or after eruption from stresses, and can become filled with organic matter from saliva (Type C).

Dentinoenamel Junction (DEJ) & Enamel SpindlesØ§Ù„Ù…Ù„ØªÙ‚Ù‰ Ø§Ù„Ù…ÙŠÙ†Ø§Ø¦ÙŠ Ø§Ù„Ø¹Ø§Ø¬ÙŠ ÙˆÙ…ØºØ§Ø²Ù„ Ø§Ù„Ù…ÙŠÙ†Ø§

The Dentinoenamel Junction (DEJ) is the interface between enamel and dentin. It has a characteristic scalloped shape, with the convexities of the scallops facing the dentin. This interlocking arrangement ensures a firm, strong attachment between the two tissues, preventing the enamel from shearing off the underlying dentin during mastication.

Enamel Spindles are short, dark, club-shaped tubules that extend from the dentin across the DEJ into the enamel. They are formed when odontoblast processes (the cell extensions that form dentin) become trapped in the developing enamel matrix before it calcifies. They are most common under the cusps of teeth.
