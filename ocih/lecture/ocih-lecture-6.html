The foundational tissues of the teeth originate from a special group of cells called Neural Crest Cells. During early embryonic development, as the neural tube (which becomes the brain and spinal cord) is forming, these cells separate and begin to migrate throughout the embryo.

The neural crest cells that migrate into the head and neck region are called cranial neural crest cells. When they mix with the embryonic connective tissue (mesenchyme), they create a new, specialized tissue called ectomesenchyme. This ectomesenchyme is crucial because it gives rise to most of the tooth's hard and soft tissues, including the odontoblasts, which are the cells responsible for forming dentin.

Tooth development is not possible without a crucial "conversation" between two types of tissues: the oral epithelium (from the ectoderm) and the underlying ectomesenchyme. This process is called epithelial-mesenchymal interaction.

This interaction is a series of programmed, sequential, and reciprocal communications. Think of it as a series of commands and responses: the epithelium sends a signal to the ectomesenchyme, which then responds, differentiates, and sends a new signal back to the epithelium. This continuous back-and-forth dialogue guides every step of odontogenesis, ensuring cells differentiate correctly and the tooth takes on its proper shape.
