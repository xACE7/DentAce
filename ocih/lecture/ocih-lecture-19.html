Dentin is classified based on when and how it is formed. The unmineralized, newly formed dentin matrix located at the pulpal border is called Predentin.

  • Secondary Dentin: Forms after the tooth root is complete.
  • Tertiary Dentin: Forms in response to injury or irritation.

Secondary DentinØ§Ù„Ø¹Ø§Ø¬ Ø§Ù„Ø«Ø§Ù†ÙˆÙŠ

Secondary dentin is a physiological deposit that forms after root completion. It is a continuation of primary dentin but is laid down at a much slower rate throughout the life of the tooth.

Key characteristics include:

  • Its tubular structure is less regular than primary dentin, and the tubules often change direction abruptly at the junction between primary and secondary dentin.
  • It is deposited unevenly, with more accumulation on the roof and floor of the pulp chamber.
  • This uneven deposition leads to a gradual reduction in the size of the pulp space, a phenomenon known as Pulp Recession.

Tertiary dentin is formed locally in response to a stimulus, such as dental caries, tooth wear, or restorative procedures. It acts as a protective barrier to shield the pulp from irritation. It is also known as response, reparative, or reactive dentin.

Unlike primary and secondary dentin, its formation is not physiological but pathological or induced. It is deposited only in the specific area under the stimulus. The structure can be highly irregular and may even lack tubules.

It is broadly divided into two types based on the severity of the stimulus:

  • Reactionary Dentin: Formed in response to a mild stimulus.
  • Reparative Dentin: Formed in response to a severe stimulus that kills the original odontoblasts.

Types of Tertiary DentinØ£Ù†ÙˆØ§Ø¹ Ø§Ù„Ø¹Ø§Ø¬ Ø§Ù„Ø«Ø§Ù„Ø«ÙŠ

1. Reactionary Dentin:

This type is formed in response to a mild stimulus where the primary odontoblasts are NOT damaged. These existing odontoblasts are stimulated to deposit dentin at a faster rate in a localized area. The resulting dentin has tubules that are less numerous and more irregular than secondary dentin, but they are continuous with the tubules of the overlying dentin.

2. Reparative Dentin:

This type is formed after an aggressive or severe stimulus (like deep caries or trauma) that causes the death of the primary odontoblasts. New odontoblast-like cells differentiate from undifferentiated mesenchymal cells (UMCs) in the pulp. These new cells then deposit reparative dentin. Its structure is highly heterogeneous and can vary significantly:

  • Atubular Dentin: Lacks dentinal tubules completely.
  • Osteodentin: Resembles bone, with entrapped cells within the matrix.
  • Vasodentin: Contains entrapped blood vessels.

With age, primary dentin itself undergoes changes that affect its permeability and appearance.

1. Sclerotic (Transparent) Dentin:

This occurs when the dentinal tubules become completely occluded (filled in) with mineral deposits. The odontoblastic processes within the tubules retract or degenerate, and the space becomes filled with calcified material. Because the tubules are filled, the refractive index of the dentin becomes uniform, causing it to appear translucent or transparent under transmitted light. This process reduces the permeability of the dentin, making it more resistant to caries.

2. Dead Tracts:

This results from a severe stimulus that leads to the death and degeneration of odontoblasts and their processes. This leaves the dentinal tubules empty and filled with air or fluid. Under transmitted light, these empty tubules reflect light differently, causing them to appear as black tracts. The pulp will often seal off these dead tracts by forming reparative dentin at the pulpal end.

Understanding the different types of dentin and their formation has significant clinical importance.

  • Pulp Recession: The continuous, slow deposition of secondary dentin reduces the size of the pulp chamber and horns over time. This makes the likelihood of pulp exposure during cavity preparation much lower in older patients compared to younger patients, who have large pulp chambers.
