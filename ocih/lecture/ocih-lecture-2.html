These secretory cells form functional units called secretory end pieces, which can have different shapes. The most common shapes are flask-like (acinus) or cylindrical (tubular). The secretions can be enzymes, hormones, or mucus, and their release is regulated by nervous and hormonal signals.

Embryological DevelopmentØ§Ù„ØªØ·ÙˆØ± Ø§Ù„Ø¬Ù†ÙŠÙ†ÙŠ

Salivary glands develop from an invagination of oral epithelium into the underlying connective tissue, known as ectomesenchyme. This epithelial cord then grows and branches.

The structure differentiates into two main parts:

  • The distal part develops into the functional secretory end pieces (acini).
  • The proximal part forms the excretory duct system, which connects the acini to the surface.

Eventually, the central cells of the branched cords degenerate to form a hollow tube, creating the lumen of the ducts and acini.

The major salivary glands begin to develop at different times during gestation:

  • Parotid Gland: Starts around the 4th to 6th week.
  • Submandibular Gland: Starts around the 6th week.
  • Sublingual and Minor Glands: Start around the 8th to 12th week.

Salivary glands are composed of two main functional parts: the parenchyma (the functional epithelial tissue) and the stroma (the supporting connective tissue).

The parenchyma consists of:

  1. Secretory End Pieces (Acini): These are clusters of cells that produce the saliva. They are classified based on the type of secretion they produce.
  2. Duct System: A network of tubes that modifies and transports the saliva from the acini to the oral cavity.

Additionally, specialized cells called Myoepithelial Cells surround the acini and parts of the duct system, playing a crucial role in secretion.

Some salivary glands contain a mix of both mucous and serous cells. In these mixed glands, the cells are often arranged in a specific structure called a serous demilune.

A serous demilune (from the French for "half-moon") is a crescent-shaped cap of serous cells that sits on top of a central mucous acinus.

The serous cells secrete their watery product into the lumen through tiny intercellular canaliculi that run between the mucous cells. Therefore, the final secretion is a mix of both mucous and serous fluids.

The Submandibular gland is a classic example of a mixed gland, containing mostly serous acini but also many mucous acini with serous demilunes.

The Duct SystemÙ†Ø¸Ø§Ù… Ø§Ù„Ù‚Ù†ÙˆØ§Øª

Saliva produced in the acini travels through a series of ducts that modify its composition before it reaches the mouth. The duct system progresses in the following order:

  1. Intercalated Ducts: The smallest ducts, leading directly from the acini. They are lined by simple cuboidal epithelium and are most prominent in serous glands.
  2. Striated (Intralobular) Ducts: Formed by the joining of several intercalated ducts. They are lined by simple columnar epithelium. Their name comes from basal "striations," which are infoldings of the basal membrane packed with mitochondria. These ducts actively modify saliva by reabsorbing sodium and secreting potassium.
  3. Interlobular (Excretory) Ducts: Located in the connective tissue septa between lobules. They are larger and lined by pseudostratified or stratified columnar epithelium.
  4. Main Duct: The final large duct that opens into the oral cavity. Its epithelium transitions from stratified columnar to stratified squamous near the opening to blend with the oral mucosa.

Both intercalated and striated ducts are located within the gland lobules and are thus called intralobular ducts.

Myoepithelial cells are specialized, contractile cells of epithelial origin. They are found situated between the secretory cells (of acini) and the basement membrane.

They are often called 'basket cells' because their long, branching processes form a basket-like network around the acini and intercalated ducts.

  • Shape: Around the acini, they have a star-like or stellate shape with multiple processes. Around the ducts, they are more elongated and spindle-shaped.
  • Composition: They contain contractile proteins like actin and myosin, similar to smooth muscle cells, which gives them their contractile ability. They also contain cytokeratin filaments, confirming their epithelial origin.
  • Function: The primary function of myoepithelial cells is to contract and squeeze the acini and ducts. This action helps to expel the saliva from the secretory end pieces into the duct system and towards the oral cavity.
