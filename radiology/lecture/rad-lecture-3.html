Radiation Biology is the scientific field that studies the effects of ionizing radiation on living systems. Understanding how radiation interacts with cells is crucial for both diagnostic and therapeutic applications in medicine.

There are two main theories explaining how radiation affects cells:

  • Hit Theory (Past Theory): This older theory suggested that radiation damage is restricted only to the cells directly "hit" by ionization, causing free radicals to form within that specific cell.
  • Bystander Effects (Current Theory): The modern understanding is more complex. Radiation causes free radicals in the "hit" cells, which then trigger cell-to-cell and cell-matrix communication. This communication spreads the damaging effects to neighboring cells that were not directly ionized.

Radiation effects on the body are categorized into two main types based on their relationship with the radiation dose:

  • Deterministic Effect: These are "definitive" damaging effects that are guaranteed to occur once a specific high dose of radiation is exceeded. The severity of the effect depends directly on the radiation dose received. A classic example is radiation mucositis (inflammation of mucous membranes).

Their occurrence is a matter of chance and does not depend on the amount of the radiation dose (there is no threshold). However, the probability of the effect occurring increases with the dose. A key example is radiation-induced cancer.

Mechanisms of Radiation Damageุขููุงุช ุงูุถุฑุฑ ุงูุฅุดุนุงุนู

Radiation causes damage to biological molecules through two primary mechanisms:

  1. Direct Effect: In this case, the energy from a photon or a secondary electron directly ionizes a crucial biological molecule like DNA. This creates a highly reactive free radical ($R^*$) from the organic molecule (RH). These radicals are unstable and quickly try to stabilize by either breaking apart (dissociation) or linking with other radicals (cross-linking). This mechanism accounts for about one-third of radiation's effects.
  2. Indirect Effect: This is the more common mechanism, responsible for about two-thirds of radiation damage. Here, the radiation ionizes water molecules in the cell, a process called radiolysis. The radiosensitivity of a cell, or how susceptible it is to radiation damage, depends on several key factors:

  • Mitotic Rate: Cells that divide rapidly are more sensitive.
  • Future Mitotic Potential: Cells that will need to divide in the future are also more sensitive.
  • Level of Differentiation: Primitive, undifferentiated cells (stem cells) are more sensitive than highly specialized, mature cells.

Based on these principles, cells can be classified by their radiosensitivity:

  1. Vegetative Intermitotic Cells: Most radiosensitive. They divide regularly and are precursor cells. Ex: Spermatogonia, erythroblasts, basal cells of oral mucosa.
  2. Differentiating Intermitotic Cells: Less sensitive. They divide less often and differentiate between divisions. Ex: Spermatocytes.
  3. Multipotential Connective Tissue Cells: Intermediate sensitivity. They divide irregularly, usually in response to demand. Ex: Fibroblasts, endothelial cells.
  4. Reverting Post-mitotic Cells: Radioresistant. They are specialized and divide infrequently. Ex: Salivary gland acinar cells, liver cells.
  5. Fixed Post-mitotic Cells: Most resistant. Highly differentiated and incapable of division once mature. Ex: Neurons, muscle cells, erythrocytes.

Radiation damage begins at the molecular level and progresses to visible cellular changes.

  • On Nucleic Acids (DNA): DNA is the most critical target in the cell. Radiation can cause change or loss of base pairs, disruption of hydrogen bonds, breakage of one or both DNA strands, and cross-linking within the DNA helix. These damages can lead to mutation, carcinogenesis, and cell death.
  • On Proteins: Radiation can alter the secondary and tertiary structures of proteins by disrupting side chains or breaking hydrogen and disulfide bonds. This can impair enzyme function.
  • On Chromosomes: Damage becomes visible during cell division. The type of aberration depends on when the cell is irradiated in the cell cycle.
    • Chromatid Aberration: Occurs if radiation hits after DNA synthesis (G2 or late S phase). Only one arm (chromatid) of the chromosome is affected.
    • Chromosome Aberration: Occurs if radiation hits before DNA replication (G1 or early S phase). Both arms of the chromosome are affected, which can lead to mutation if repaired improperly.
  • On Cytoplasm: Organelles like mitochondria are more resistant, requiring high doses (30-50 Gy) to show damage, such as swelling and increased permeability.

Radiation therapy to the head and neck region can cause significant oral complications. These are divided into acute (short-term) and chronic (long-term) effects.

Oral Mucous Membrane

The basal cell layer of the mucosa is highly radiosensitive. During the second week of therapy, these cells die, causing the membrane to break down. This leads to mucositis, characterized by a painful white or yellow pseudomembrane. Healing usually completes within 2 months, but the mucosa may remain thin and atrophic. Treatment involves good oral hygiene and topical anesthetics like Benzocaine.

Taste Buds

Taste is affected in the second to third week of therapy due to direct damage to taste buds or indirectly from hyposalivation. Taste function typically returns to normal in 2-4 months, though very high doses can cause permanent loss. Zinc supplementation may be helpful.

Salivary Glands

Serous acini are more radiosensitive than mucous acini, making the parotid gland particularly vulnerable. Radiation causes inflammation, progressive fibrosis, and parenchymal degeneration, leading to xerostomia (dry mouth). This reduces saliva's buffering capacity, increases the risk of candidiasis, and causes difficulty with speech and swallowing. Management includes saliva substitutes and sialogogues like Pilocarpine.

Teeth

The effect on teeth depends on the stage of development at the time of irradiation.

  • Before Development: If radiation occurs before tooth development begins, the tooth bud can be destroyed entirely.
  • During Calcification: Radiation can cause tooth malformations, retarded root development, and dwarfed teeth.
  • After Development: Fully formed teeth are not directly damaged, but they become highly susceptible to radiation caries. This is not due to radiation harming the tooth structure, but due to the hostile oral environment caused by xerostomia (hyposalivation, increased pH, loss of buffering capacity).

Bone

Radiation severely damages the vasculature of the periosteum and cortical bone, leading to a state of hypovascularity, hypoxia, and hypocellularity. It destroys osteoblasts and osteoclasts, and the marrow is replaced by fibrous and fatty tissue. This compromised bone has a very poor healing capacity and is at high risk of developing osteoradionecrosis (bone death) following trauma, such as a tooth extraction.

Mandibular Dysfunction

Fibrosis of the muscles of mastication can occur as a late effect of radiation, leading to trismus (difficulty opening the mouth) and other musculoskeletal problems.

  • Radiation Biology studies the effects of ionizing radiation on living systems. Damage occurs via Direct Effects (1/3) and more commonly, Indirect Effects (2/3) through the radiolysis of water to form free radicals.
