Intra-oral radiographs are images taken by placing a film packet or sensor inside the patient's mouth. The X-ray beam is then projected from outside the mouth at various angles to capture the image.

There are three main types of intra-oral radiographs:

  • Intra-oral Periapical Radiograph (IOPA): Shows the entire tooth, from the crown to the apex of the root, and the supporting bone.
  • Bitewing Radiograph: Captures the crowns of both maxillary and mandibular teeth in one image, primarily used to detect interproximal caries.
  • Occlusal Radiograph: Provides a wider view of the entire arch of teeth, useful for examining large areas of the maxilla or mandible.

Periapical radiography is designed to capture a detailed view of a specific tooth or group of teeth. The term itself gives a clue to its purpose: 'Peri' means around, and 'apical' refers to the apex or tip of the root.

There are two primary techniques used to take periapical radiographs:

  1. Bisecting Angle Technique: Also known as the Short Cone Technique or Cieszynski's Technique.
  2. Paralleling Technique: Also known as the Long Cone Technique.

Each technique has its own principles, advantages, and disadvantages, and the choice between them often depends on the clinical situation and practitioner's preference.

The Bisecting Angle Technique is based on a geometric principle called Cieszynski's Rule of Isometry. This rule states that two triangles are equal if they share one complete side and have two equal angles.

In this technique, the radiographic film is placed as close to the tooth as possible. Since the film and the long axis of the tooth are not parallel, they form an angle. The core principle is to direct the central X-ray beam perpendicular (at 90Â°) to an imaginary bisector that divides this angle in half.

By following this principle, the length of the tooth's image on the film will be the same as the actual length of the tooth, thus avoiding distortion like elongation or foreshortening.

The Paralleling Technique, also known as the long cone technique, is considered by many to be superior to the bisecting angle technique as it produces images with less geometric distortion.

The fundamental principles are:

  1. The film (or sensor) is placed in the mouth parallel to the long axis of the tooth being radiographed.
  2. The central X-ray beam is directed perpendicular (at a right angle) to both the film and the long axis of the tooth.

To achieve this parallelism, the film must be placed further away from the tooth, often towards the middle of the oral cavity. This increased distance is compensated for by using a long cone (or PID) to maintain image sharpness and prevent magnification. This requires the use of a film holder to stabilize the film in the correct parallel position.

The two main techniques for object localization are the Parallax technique and the Right Angle technique.

The Parallax method, developed by Charles A. Clark in 1909, is also known as the Tube-shift Technique or Clark's Rule. It works based on the principle of parallax: the apparent displacement of an object when the viewpoint changes.

This technique requires taking two periapical radiographs of the same area. For the second image, the horizontal angulation of the X-ray tube is shifted either mesially or distally.

The rule to interpret the images is the famous mnemonic SLOB - Same Lingual, Opposite Buccal:

  • If the object in question appears to move in the SAME direction as the tube shift, it is located on the LINGUAL side.
  • If the object appears to move in the OPPOSITE direction of the tube shift, it is located on the BUCCAL side.
