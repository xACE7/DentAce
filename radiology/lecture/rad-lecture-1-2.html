Atoms can combine to form a molecule by transferring or sharing electrons from their outer shells. When an atom gains or loses an electron, it becomes electrically unbalanced and is called an ion. X-rays have a very short wavelength, which gives them high frequency and high energy, allowing them to penetrate matter.

When high-speed electrons from the cathode strike the tungsten target of the anode, their kinetic energy is converted into X-ray photons through two primary mechanisms:

  1. Bremsstrahlung (Braking) Radiation: This is the primary source of X-rays (about 70%). It occurs when a high-speed electron is slowed down and deflected by the positive charge of the tungsten atom's nucleus. The kinetic energy lost by the "braking" electron is emitted as an X-ray photon. This process produces a continuous spectrum of X-ray energies.
  2. Characteristic Radiation: This occurs when a high-speed electron from the filament collides with and ejects an inner-shell electron (e.g., from the K-shell) of a tungsten atom. This leaves a vacancy, which is immediately filled by an electron from an outer shell. As the outer-shell electron drops to a lower energy level, it releases a specific amount of energy in the form of an X-ray photon. The energy is "characteristic" of the target material (tungsten).

Tube Voltage (kVp) Kilovolt peak. Controls the speed of electrons traveling from cathode to anode. Controls the quality (penetrating power) of the X-ray beam. Higher kVp = more energy, more penetration.

Filtration Aluminum discs placed in the path of the beam. Improves quality by removing low-energy, less penetrating X-rays, which reduces patient skin dose.
Collimation A lead diaphragm that restricts the size and shape of the X-ray beam. Controls the size of the beam, reducing patient exposure by limiting the area irradiated.
Inverse Square Law Physical principle relating distance and beam intensity. States that the intensity of the beam is inversely proportional to the square of the distance from the source. Doubling the distance reduces intensity to one-quarter.

  • Coherent Scattering (Thomson Effect): A low-energy incident photon passes near an outer-shell electron, causing it to vibrate.
